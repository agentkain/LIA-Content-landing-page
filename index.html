What are "forever chemicals?"

"Forever chemicals" are substances with super strong chemical bonds that don't break down easily. This means things like heat, fire, water, sunlight or bacteria can't break them apart.

What do PFAS chemicals do to people?

Cancer

PFAS, forever chemicals, are linked to prostate, kidney and testicular cancers—and many others.

Problems getting pregnant

These "forever chemicals" may interfere with your hormones, reducing fertility for both men and women.

Hormonal issues

In addition to sex hormones, PFAS may impact thyroid, digestive and metabolic, growth, mood and other hormones that regulate body systems.

Liver damage

Long-term exposure to PFAS may lead to liver disease.

Problems with your immune system

PFAS may disrupt the development of antibodies and lead to hypersensitivity disorders, like asthma and allergies.

High cholesterol

Exposure to PFAS is linked to elevated levels of blood fats, like cholesterol.

Mood and behavior problems

Evidence of the neurotoxicity of PFAS links them to mood, sleep and behavior issues.

Developmental issues

PFAS may affect the development of children before and after birth.

FAQs

What are chemicals that cannot be broken down?

Often called "forever chemicals," these substances have incredibly strong chemical bonds that don't easily break down. PFAS are a well-known example, lasting for decades in the environment and in our bodies.

Can cancer be triggered by food and water?

If the food and water you consume are contaminated with PFAS, your risk of developing certain types of cancer may increase. These chemicals have been found in drinking water, soil and food packaging, making exposure hard to avoid.

What types of cancer do PFAS cause?

Scientists are studying how PFAS may trigger various cancers. So far, research has found links to kidney cancer, testicular cancer and breast cancer, among others.

What's the difference between PFAS, PFOS and PFOA?

PFAS is the larger group of "forever chemicals." Two specific types of PFAS are PFOS, found in products like Scotchgard, and PFOA, used in Teflon. They have different chemical structures and uses, but both are harmful and long-lasting.

Can you get rid of "forever chemicals" in your body?

Removing PFAS from your body is very difficult. The best way to protect yourself is to reduce exposure by avoiding products that contain PFAS and drinking clean water. Eating antioxidant-rich foods, supporting kidney and liver health and getting plenty of fiber and water can help your body flush out toxins. Exercise and sweating, such as through workouts or saunas, may also support your body's natural detox process.

How much PFAS is dangerous to your body?

There's no clear answer yet because there are so many types of PFAS and not enough long-term studies. Some health organizations have set advisory levels, but even small amounts of these chemicals in your body can be harmful.

Are PFAS the same as microplastics?

No, they're different. PFAS are chemicals, while microplastics are tiny physical particles of plastic. Both are man-made pollutants, but they affect the environment and human health differently.
