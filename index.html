PFAS: Toxic, Forever Chemicals

Polyfluoroalkyl substances, or PFAS, are chemicals that resist heat, water, oil and stains. These qualities make them incredibly useful in many everyday products. However, PFAS are often called "forever chemicals" because they don't break down over time. Instead, they build up in the environment and in our bodies, earning them another nickname: "everywhere chemicals." Unfortunately, their build-up can lead to serious health problems. Forever chemicals have extremely strong bonds between carbon and fluorine atoms, making them resistant to breaking down in the environment or living organisms.

What are "forever chemicals?"

"Forever chemicals" are substances with super strong chemical bonds that don't break down easily. This means things like heat, fire, water, sunlight or bacteria can't break them apart. Because "forever chemicals" last so long, they can build up in the environment, animals and even people, causing serious harm over time.

Understanding PFOA and PFOS

PFOA and PFOS are two major types of PFAS "forever chemicals." They're used in many products for their ability to resist oil, water, stains, and heat. Common sources include:

  • Food packaging
  • Stain-resistant carpets and fabrics
  • Nonstick cookware
  • Personal care products
  • Soil and water (from pollution)
  • Treated waste fertilizers
PFOA and PFOS are two specific types of PFAS chemicals that have been widely used in consumer products. Their molecular structure makes them excellent at repelling water and oil.

While companies are phasing out PFOA and PFOS due to cancer risks, replacement chemicals like HFPO and PFBS may be equally concerning. Early research suggests these alternatives could be just as harmful, highlighting the ongoing challenges in finding safe solutions.

What are PFAS?

PFAS, short for perfluoroalkyl and polyfluoroalkyl substances, are human-made (synthetic) chemicals designed for specific purposes. They're manufactured by bonding carbon and fluorine atoms, creating one of the strongest chemical bonds found in everyday products. Think of it like superglue for chemicals—the bond is so strong that PFAS doesn't easily react with other substances. This makes PFAS great for repelling water, oil and grease, as well as resisting fire and sticking.

The chemical structure of PFAS shows the strong bonds between carbon and fluorine atoms that give these substances their persistent properties and resistance to breaking down.

Why do PFAS matter to you?

While these qualities make PFAS useful in products like nonstick pans, waterproof clothing and firefighting foam, they also have a serious downside: PFAS don't break down. This means they stay in our environment and bodies. Over time, PFAS can build up and cause health problems, including cancer and other serious illnesses.

Why are PFAS still used?

Even though PFAS are dangerous, they're still used because finding replacements is challenging. However, scientists and companies are working hard to develop safer alternatives.

PFAS use began in the 1930s and 1940s with discoveries like polychlorotrifluoroethylene (PCTFE) by German scientists and Teflon by Roy J. Plunkett at DuPont. Since then, the use of PFAS has exploded across many industries.

The hidden danger

Shockingly, these industries knew about the risks of PFAS as early as the 1940s and 1950s but didn't share this information. Over the years, the harmful effects became impossible to hide. Now, companies like 3M, DuPont, Chemours and Corteva are facing lawsuits for the damage caused by PFAS and "forever chemicals."

The ongoing problem

Today, more dangers from different types of PFAS are still being discovered. For example, trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), a type of PFAS, has been found in drinking water, adding to the growing list of concerns.

PFAS may have been designed to make life easier, but their lasting impact on our health and environment shows they come at a serious cost.

People more likely to be affected by PFAS

You can be exposed to PFAS in many ways. One of the most common is through drinking water. PFAS can seep into groundwater from industrial sites, landfills and places where firefighting foam has been used. A study by the U.S. Geological Survey found that about 45% of tap water in the U.S. contains at least one type of "forever chemical."

PFAS can also leak into soil, affecting the plants and animals we eat. Even the air we breathe can carry tiny PFAS particles from dust or industrial pollution.

Some groups of people and places are more likely to be affected by PFAS, including:

  • Firefighters
  • Workers in chemical manufacturing
  • People living near factories or industrial sites
  • Residents in urban areas
  • Communities near landfills
  • Children
  • Communities of color
  • People living near military bases

What's being done to stop PFAS "forever chemicals?"

Efforts to protect our health, the environment and all living things from PFAS have been slow, but progress is speeding up. The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has created a strategic roadmap to tackle the issue, guided by these key goals:

Learning more about PFAS

Scientists are studying how PFAS are made, used and disposed of, how they move through the environment and how people are exposed to them. Think of it as mapping out the journey of these "forever chemicals" to understand where they cause harm.

Reducing contamination and exposure

Steps are being taken to limit how much PFAS enters the environment and how many people come into contact with it. This includes cleaning up polluted areas and regulating PFAS use.

Holding responsible parties accountable

Companies and industries that make, use or improperly dispose of PFAS are held responsible for the harm they cause to people and the environment.

Improving scientific understanding

Research is ongoing to better understand PFAS and its effects. This knowledge helps create smarter policies and stronger protections for everyone.

Protecting disadvantaged communities

Special attention is being given to communities that face the greatest risks from PFAS exposure, such as low-income areas or communities near industrial sites, to ensure they are prioritized for protection.

While there's still work to do, these steps are helping to create a healthier and safer future for everyone.

FAQs

What are chemicals that cannot be broken down?

Often called "forever chemicals," these substances have incredibly strong chemical bonds that don't easily break down. PFAS are a well-known example, lasting for decades in the environment and in our bodies.

Can cancer be triggered by food and water?

If the food and water you consume are contaminated with PFAS, your risk of developing certain types of cancer may increase. These chemicals have been found in drinking water, soil and food packaging, making exposure hard to avoid.

What types of cancer do PFAS cause?

Scientists are studying how PFAS may trigger various cancers. So far, research has found links to kidney cancer, testicular cancer and breast cancer, among others.

What's the difference between PFAS, PFOS and PFOA?

PFAS is the larger group of "forever chemicals." Two specific types of PFAS are PFOS, found in products like Scotchgard, and PFOA, used in Teflon. They have different chemical structures and uses, but both are harmful and long-lasting.

Can you get rid of "forever chemicals" in your body?

Removing PFAS from your body is very difficult. The best way to protect yourself is to reduce exposure by avoiding products that contain PFAS and drinking clean water. Eating antioxidant-rich foods, supporting kidney and liver health and getting plenty of fiber and water can help your body flush out toxins. Exercise and sweating, such as through workouts or saunas, may also support your body's natural detox process.

How much PFAS is dangerous to your body?

There's no clear answer yet because there are so many types of PFAS and not enough long-term studies. Some health organizations have set advisory levels, but even small amounts of these chemicals in your body can be harmful.

Are PFAS the same as microplastics?

No, they're different. PFAS are chemicals, while microplastics are tiny physical particles of plastic. Both are man-made pollutants, but they affect the environment and human health differently.
